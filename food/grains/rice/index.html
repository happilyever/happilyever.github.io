Rice

Rice is the most widely consumed staple food for a large part of the world’s human population, especially in Asia. Rice is the most important grain with regard to human nutrition and caloric intake, providing more than one-fifth of the calories consumed worldwide by humans.

While growing, rice plants tend to absorb arsenic more readily than other food crops, causes possible arsenic-related risks associated with rice consumption. The amount of arsenic in rice varies widely with the greatest concentration in brown rice and rice grown on land formerly used to grow cotton, possibly caused by past use of arsenic-based pesticides to control cotton weevils.

Cooked rice may contain Bacillus cereus spores, which produce an emetic toxin at room temperature. When storing cooked rice for use the next day, rapid cooling is advised to reduce the risk of toxin production.

Brown Rice

Brown rice such as Jasmine or Basmati still contain their germ and bran layers, meaning they provide a range of important nutrients including B vitamins, phosphorus, and magnesium. Exchanging White Rice for Brown May Help Lower Your Type 2 Diabetes Risk.

Black Rice

Black Rice is cultivated in China. The bran layer of black rice contains a surfeit of anthocyanins. These are the same type of antioxidants, found in dark berries such as blueberries and blackberries.

White Rice

Rice consists of three components: the endosperm, the bran, and the germ. The bran is where much of the dietary fiber is found. The germ harbors a bundle of vitamins and minerals. When brown rice is processed to make white rice, the bran and germ are removed, leaving behind what’s mainly just endosperm. After a vigorous workout, your body can better benefit from the fast-digesting carbohydrates white rice provides.
